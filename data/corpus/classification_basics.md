# Variant Classification: the 5-tier system

> Condensed development sample. See the ACMG/AMP 2015 guideline for the authoritative text.

When a genetic variant is found, the clinical question is whether it causes disease. The ACMG/AMP
framework answers this on a five-point scale:

- **Pathogenic:** strong evidence the variant causes disease.
- **Likely Pathogenic:** evidence favors disease-causing, but with less certainty (roughly 90
  percent or greater confidence).
- **Uncertain Significance (VUS):** the evidence is insufficient or conflicting. The correct
  action is not to over-call it as pathogenic or benign.
- **Likely Benign:** evidence favors harmless, with less certainty.
- **Benign:** strong evidence the variant does not cause disease.

Pathogenic and Likely Pathogenic are usually managed the same way clinically, as are Benign and
Likely Benign. The hardest and most common real-world case is the VUS: a variant that has not yet
accumulated enough evidence to land on either side.

Two kinds of evidence drive a classification. **Population frequency:** a variant common in the
general population is unlikely to cause a rare disease, so high frequency points benign. **Variant
consequence:** a variant that truncates a protein in a gene where loss of function causes disease
points pathogenic. The final call weighs all available evidence using the ACMG criteria.
